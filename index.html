01

What is a Cleanroom?

A cleanroom is a controlled environment designed to maintain extremely low levels of airborne particulates, microorganisms, and other contaminants. It achieves this through a combination of architectural design, pressurisation strategies, material selection, and — most critically — a precisely engineered HVAC (Heating, Ventilation and Air Conditioning) system.

Cleanrooms are not a single standard — they exist on a spectrum of cleanliness classifications, each with strict requirements on particle count, temperature, humidity, air change rates, and pressurisation. The environment must be continuously maintained and monitored.

Industry Context

Cleanrooms are the backbone of Singapore's three most critical high-technology industries: Semiconductor manufacturing, Pharmaceutical production, and increasingly, the mechanical rooms supporting hyperscale Data Centers. Understanding cleanroom HVAC is foundational to working in any of these sectors.

ISO Classification Standards

The international benchmark for cleanroom classification is ISO 14644-1, which replaced the older US Federal Standard 209E. It defines cleanliness classes based on the maximum allowable concentration of airborne particles of specific sizes per cubic metre of air.

03

Why HVAC is Critical in Cleanrooms

In a standard commercial building, HVAC primarily controls thermal comfort. In a cleanroom, HVAC serves as the primary contamination control mechanism. It must simultaneously achieve multiple, often competing engineering objectives:

  • ✓
    Particle removal — Continuous air filtration through HEPA/ULPA filters to dilute and exhaust airborne particles generated by people, equipment, and processes.
  • ✓
    Pressurisation control — Maintaining positive pressure differentials between zones to prevent cross-contamination and uncontrolled air infiltration.
  • ✓
    Temperature stability — Precise temperature control (often ±0.5°C or tighter) to prevent thermal expansion effects on precision semiconductor manufacturing tools.
  • ✓
    Humidity control — Controlling relative humidity to prevent electrostatic discharge (ESD) in electronics manufacturing or microbial growth in pharmaceutical environments.
  • ✓
    Airflow uniformity — Ensuring laminar or turbulent airflow patterns are consistent and predictable to prevent dead zones where particles accumulate.
  • ✓
    Chemical exhaust management — Safe exhaust of process chemicals (acids, solvents, gases) used in semiconductor fabrication and pharmaceutical manufacturing.
04

Airflow Design Principles

There are two fundamental airflow regimes used in cleanroom HVAC design, and the choice between them is directly tied to the ISO classification target:

🌊
Unidirectional (Laminar) Flow
ISO Class 1–5
Air flows in parallel streams from ceiling HEPA/ULPA filter arrays down to raised floor grilles. Velocity typically 0.3–0.5 m/s. Used in critical process zones (wafer fab bays, filling lines).
🌀
Non-Unidirectional (Turbulent) Flow
ISO Class 6–8
Air is mixed and diluted throughout the space using high-volume air change rates. More cost-effective, suitable for less critical zones, corridors, and gowning areas.

"In semiconductor fab design, the transition from non-unidirectional to unidirectional zones is one of the most critical interface points in the HVAC layout — getting it wrong can compromise the entire process environment."

ZABU Asia Engineering — Field Experience Note

Many real-world cleanrooms use a mixed approach — unidirectional flow over critical process tools (tool mini-environments or FOUPs in semiconductor fabs) combined with non-unidirectional background environments, reducing energy consumption significantly while protecting the critical zones.

Key system components explained:

  • →
    MAU (Make-Up Air Unit) — Conditions 100% outside air for ventilation and pressurisation. Typically handles dehumidification, pre-cooling, and pre-heating before mixing with recirculated air.
  • →
    Recirculation AHU (RAHU) — Circulates large volumes of filtered air within the cleanroom to achieve the required air change rates. These units handle the bulk of the cooling load from process tools and lighting.
  • →
    HEPA Filters (H14) — High Efficiency Particulate Air filters capturing 99.995% of particles ≥0.3µm. Mounted in filter fan units (FFUs) or ceiling plenums directly above the cleanroom.
  • →
    ULPA Filters (U15/U16) — Ultra-Low Penetration Air filters for ISO Class 1–3. Captures 99.9995% of particles ≥0.12µm. Higher static pressure than HEPA — requires careful fan selection.
  • →
    Chilled Water System (CWS) — Provides cooling to the AHUs and process cooling equipment. Typical supply temperatures of 6–7°C chilled water for comfort, and lower for process applications.
  • →
    Process Exhaust System — Dedicated exhaust for hazardous chemicals, typically divided into acid exhaust, solvent exhaust, and general exhaust streams, each with separate treatment before atmospheric release.

Key Design Parameters

The following engineering parameters are the core inputs and outputs of any cleanroom HVAC design. Engineers must understand the relationships between these values and how changes in one parameter propagate through the entire system design.

🌡️
Temperature Setpoint
21°C ± 0.5°C
Typical semiconductor fab. Pharma may accept ±2°C. Tighter tolerances require larger HVAC systems.
💧
Relative Humidity
40–50% RH
Semiconductor: low RH for ESD control. Pharma: controlled for microbial prevention. Singapore climate makes dehumidification a major energy load.
🔁
Air Change Rate
60–600+ ACH
ISO 8: ~60 ACH. ISO 5: 250–600 ACH. ISO 3–4 semiconductor bays may exceed 600 ACH with unidirectional flow systems.
🔵
Filter Coverage (HEPA)
25–100%
ISO 8: ~25% ceiling coverage. ISO 5: 100% HEPA ceiling. Percentage directly impacts construction and energy costs.
⬆️
Room Pressurisation
+12.5 Pa typical
Positive pressure maintained relative to adjacent lower-class areas. Cascade pressure zoning is designed room by room across the facility.
💨
Unidirectional Velocity
0.3–0.5 m/s
Measured 150–300mm below the HEPA face. Below 0.3 m/s risks turbulence and particle accumulation; above 0.5 m/s increases energy costs.
Air Change Rate (ACH) = (Supply Airflow Rate m³/hr) ÷ (Room Volume m³) Example: 10,000 m³/hr supply into a 100 m³ room = 100 ACH. Always verify against the ISO class target and particle balance calculation.
HEPA Filter Coverage (%) = (Total Filter Face Area m²) ÷ (Cleanroom Ceiling Area m²) × 100 For unidirectional flow zones targeting ISO 4–5, aim for 80–100% coverage. For turbulent zones (ISO 7–8), 25–35% is typical for new designs.
07

Design Codes & Standards

Cleanroom HVAC design is governed by an overlapping set of international and local standards. Singapore engineers must be familiar with the applicable codes for their specific project type:

  • →
    ISO 14644-1/2 — Classification of air cleanliness by particle concentration, and monitoring requirements. The primary global standard for all cleanroom classifications.
  • →
    ISO 14644-4 — Design, construction and start-up of cleanrooms and associated controlled environments.
  • →
    EU GMP Annex 1 (2022 revised) — Manufacture of Sterile Medicinal Products. Critical for pharmaceutical cleanroom design in Singapore regulated by HSA. The 2022 revision significantly expanded HVAC and contamination control requirements.
  • →
    ASHRAE 170 — Ventilation of Health Care Facilities. Referenced for pharmaceutical sterile manufacturing environments and hospital cleanrooms.
  • →
    SEMI S2 / S14 — Environmental, health, and safety guidelines for semiconductor manufacturing equipment. Governs exhaust system design and chemical safety.
  • →
    SS 553 (Singapore Standard) — Code of Practice for Air-Conditioning and Mechanical Ventilation in Buildings. The Singapore baseline ACMV standard applicable to all building projects including cleanroom facilities.
  • →
    BCA Green Mark — Singapore's building energy rating system. Cleanroom energy efficiency design increasingly required to meet Green Mark requirements for new facilities.
💡 Note for Engineers

In Singapore's MNC project environment, you will typically encounter a hierarchy of standards — the MNC client's own internal engineering standards take precedence, followed by the applicable international standards (ISO/ASHRAE/SEMI), and finally the Singapore local codes (SS 553, BCA). Understanding how to navigate conflicts between these layers is a key practical skill.

08

Common Design Challenges

Cleanroom HVAC design involves solving a set of recurring engineering challenges that are rarely covered in academic curricula but are critical in real project delivery:

  • !
    Process heat load uncertainty — Semiconductor process tools can have highly variable heat loads. Designing for peak loads is expensive; designing too lean risks thermal upsets. Dynamic load profiling and spare cooling capacity strategy is critical.
  • !
    Dehumidification in tropical climates — Singapore's year-round high humidity makes latent load management a dominant design challenge. MAU sizing, chilled water temperatures, and reheat coil design must be carefully coordinated.
  • !
    Pressurisation cascading — Maintaining correct pressure differentials across 10–30+ zones simultaneously while managing variable exhaust and supply airflows is a complex control engineering challenge.
  • !
    Construction phasing and contamination — Cleanrooms are often built in phases while adjacent areas remain operational. Managing construction contamination risks to live cleanroom zones requires careful HVAC zoning strategies during construction.
  • !
    Energy vs. cleanliness trade-off — Achieving ISO Class 3–4 is extremely energy-intensive. Innovative strategies like variable air volume (VAV) systems, occupancy-based ACH reduction, and FFU speed control are increasingly used to reduce energy consumption without compromising cleanliness.
  • !
    Vibration and acoustic control — High-volume air circulation systems generate significant vibration and noise. Precision semiconductor tools have strict vibration criteria (VC-A to VC-E curves), requiring careful isolation of HVAC equipment and ductwork.
From the Field

Many of these challenges are not visible in design drawings — they emerge during commissioning and qualification. Engineers who have lived through cleanroom construction projects understand that the gap between design intent and operational reality is where the real learning happens.
