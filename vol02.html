01

What is Cleanroom Pressurisation?

Cleanroom pressurisation is the engineering practice of maintaining the air pressure inside a cleanroom space at a level that is higher (positive) or lower (negative) than adjacent spaces. This pressure difference — known as the differential pressure — creates a controlled airflow direction whenever a door or gap exists between two zones.

The concept is straightforward: air always flows from high pressure to low pressure. By engineering which zone is at higher pressure, engineers control the direction that air — and therefore airborne particles and contaminants — can travel.

Key Concept

Pressurisation does not filter air — it directs air movement. It works alongside HEPA filtration and air change rates as the third layer of contamination control in a cleanroom. All three must work together for effective environmental control.

02

Why Pressure Differentials Matter

Every time a cleanroom door opens, corridor door swings, or an air gap exists around penetrations, there is an opportunity for uncontrolled air movement. Without pressurisation control, contaminated air from lower-class areas can infiltrate higher-class cleanroom spaces — defeating the entire purpose of the filtration system.

The consequences of pressurisation failure depend on the industry:

💾
Semiconductor Impact
Yield Loss
Particle infiltration on wafers causes defects, killing chips in production. A single contamination event can result in millions of dollars of product loss in a high-volume fab.
💊
Pharmaceutical Impact
Batch Failure
Microbial or particulate contamination of sterile products can cause full batch rejection, regulatory action by HSA/FDA/EMA, and in worst cases, patient safety risk.
🔬
Negative Pressure Use
Containment
Hazardous chemical areas, potent compound handling (OEB4/5), and BSL laboratories use negative pressure to prevent outward leakage of dangerous materials.
📋
Regulatory Impact
Audit Failure
HSA, FDA, and EMA auditors specifically review pressurisation records during GMP inspections. Inadequate monitoring data is a common 483 observation.

"Pressurisation is the invisible shield of a cleanroom. You cannot see it working — but you will immediately see the consequences when it fails."

How Cascade Zoning Works

Cascade zoning is the systematic arrangement of cleanroom spaces at progressively decreasing pressure levels as you move from the cleanest (most critical) zone outward to the less clean zones and finally to the general building or outside environment.

The design logic is simple: the highest pressure is maintained in the cleanest zone. Air always leaks outward — never inward — protecting the critical environment at all times.

Each step down in the cascade is typically 10–15 Pa. This step size must be large enough to be robust against normal HVAC fluctuations and door operations, but not so large that it creates excessive airflow leakage that wastes energy and makes the system difficult to control.

💡 Design Principle

In cascade design, airlocks and anterooms are not just architectural spaces — they are active pressure buffer zones. An airlock between a gowning room and the cleanroom means that at no point are the general building and the cleanroom directly connected, even when both doors are momentarily open.

04

Semiconductor Fab Cascade Design

Semiconductor fabs are among the most complex cleanroom pressurisation challenges in the world. A large fab may have 20–50 individually pressurised zones across multiple floors, with a mix of positive-pressure process areas and negative-pressure chemical exhaust zones — all of which must be managed simultaneously.

  • →
    Fab Floor (ISO 4–5, +50 Pa) — The main wafer processing floor, maintained at the highest positive pressure. All airflow from surrounding areas is directed outward, away from the wafers.
  • →
    Sub-fab / Mechanical Chase (ISO 6–7, +40 Pa) — Located directly below the fab floor. Houses process tool connections, exhausts, and utility systems. Slightly lower pressure than the fab above.
  • →
    Chemical Storage Rooms (Negative Pressure) — Bulk chemical storage and hazardous gas cabinet areas are maintained at negative pressure to prevent outward leakage of toxic or flammable vapours.
  • →
    Corridor / Support Areas (ISO 7–8, +25–30 Pa) — General support corridors, maintenance access, and lower-classification process areas form the intermediate buffer ring around the core fab.
  • →
    Gowning Rooms (+15–20 Pa) — Entry control zones where personnel transition between general building clothing and cleanroom garments. Pressure is stepped down from the fab side to the building side.
  • →
    CDA / N₂ Rooms (Positive, Isolated) — Compressed Dry Air and Nitrogen distribution rooms are typically maintained at slight positive pressure but are isolated from the main fab pressurisation cascade.
⚠️ Critical Design Challenge

In semiconductor fabs, chemical exhaust systems create strong negative pressure pull on process tools. This conflicts with the positive pressurisation of the fab floor. Balancing these opposing forces — especially under variable process exhaust flow conditions — is one of the most demanding HVAC control engineering challenges in the industry. It requires very careful airflow balancing and fast-responding VAV control systems.

Pharmaceutical Cascade Design

Pharmaceutical cleanrooms follow a graded zone system defined by EU GMP Annex 1 (2022 revision) and aligned with ISO 14644-1 classifications. Unlike semiconductor fabs which focus on particle control, pharmaceutical facilities must additionally manage microbial contamination — which means the cascade design also considers personnel flow, material airlocks, and gowning protocols.

A key pharmaceutical-specific requirement is the material airlock and personnel airlock — these are separate, dedicated transition spaces with interlocked doors (only one door can open at a time) to maintain the pressure cascade integrity during movement of people and materials into the cleanroom.

💡 EU GMP Annex 1 (2022) Update

The 2022 revision of EU GMP Annex 1 significantly strengthened requirements around Contamination Control Strategy (CCS) documentation, which now explicitly requires a written justification for all pressurisation design decisions. Singapore's HSA has adopted alignment with EU GMP Annex 1 for sterile pharmaceutical manufacturing. Engineers working on pharma projects must be familiar with this revised standard.

06

Pressure Control Strategies

Maintaining stable pressure differentials in a live cleanroom is an active, continuous control challenge. The HVAC system must respond to constant disturbances — door openings, variable process exhaust flows, changes in occupancy, and seasonal weather variations — while keeping pressure within tight tolerances.

Control Method How it Works Best Used For Complexity
Fixed Flow (Constant Volume) Supply and exhaust airflows are set at fixed values during commissioning. Pressure results from the imbalance between supply and exhaust. Simple, small cleanrooms with stable loads Low
Pressure Sensor + VAV Control Differential pressure sensors monitor each zone. VAV dampers on supply or exhaust ducts modulate to maintain pressure setpoint. Most modern cleanrooms — pharmaceutical and semiconductor Medium
Cascade Control (Master-Slave) A master controller manages the highest-pressure zone; downstream zones are controlled relative to the zone above them in the cascade. Multi-zone facilities with complex cascade requirements Medium-High
Active Exhaust Tracking Supply airflow tracks and responds to changes in process exhaust volume in real time, maintaining the supply-exhaust differential needed for pressurisation. Semiconductor fabs with variable chemical exhaust loads High
Airlock Interlocking Door interlock controls ensure only one airlock door can open at a time, limiting the duration and magnitude of pressure upset during personnel/material entry. Pharmaceutical Grade A/B zones, potent compound areas Low (add-on)
Required Supply-Exhaust Differential (m³/hr) = Leakage Area (m²) × Velocity through gap (m/s) × 3600 The leakage area is the sum of all gaps — door undercuts, penetrations, and cracks — between the pressurised zone and adjacent spaces. In practice, this is estimated using room pressurisation test data or leakage coefficients from standards like ISO 14644-4 Annex B.
Pressure Differential (Pa) = 0.5 × ρ × v² Where ρ = air density (~1.2 kg/m³ at 20°C) and v = air velocity through the gap (m/s). This relationship shows that doubling the gap velocity quadruples the pressure differential — small gap sizes have a large impact on pressure stability.
07

Instrumentation & Monitoring

Pressurisation without reliable measurement is not cleanroom design — it is guesswork. Every cleanroom cascade zone must be instrumented, monitored, and alarmed, with data recorded for regulatory compliance and operational troubleshooting.

  • ✓
    Magnehelic Gauges (Visual, Local) — Simple analogue differential pressure gauges mounted on walls between zones. Provide immediate visual indication for operators. Inexpensive but require manual reading and provide no data logging.
  • ✓
    Electronic Differential Pressure Transmitters (DPT) — 4–20mA or digital output sensors connected to the Building Management System (BMS/BAS). Enable real-time monitoring, alarming, data trending, and control system integration. Industry standard for all modern cleanrooms.
  • ✓
    BMS Alarm Setpoints — Typically configured with two alarm levels: a Low alarm (e.g. pressure drops below 5 Pa) triggering an advisory alert, and a Critical alarm (e.g. pressure reversal or <2 Pa) triggering immediate investigation. Alarm response procedures must be documented in SOPs.
  • ✓
    Environmental Monitoring Systems (EMS) — In pharmaceutical facilities, pressure monitoring data must be captured by a validated EMS system (e.g. Vaisala viewLinc, Kaye Validator) with CFR 21 Part 11-compliant audit trails for regulatory submission.
  • ✓
    Data Logging & Trending — Historical pressure data allows engineers to identify drift, seasonal variations, and the impact of maintenance activities on pressurisation performance. Required for pharmaceutical batch records and GMP compliance.
💡 Singapore Practice Note

In Singapore MNC projects, the DPT sensor specification — including accuracy class, calibration interval, and BMS integration protocol — is typically defined in the client's Basis of Design (BOD) document. Engineers must ensure sensor placements are agreed during the design review stage, as sensor locations affect both the accuracy of monitoring and the regulatory defensibility of the data.

Common Design Mistakes

Many pressurisation problems in operational cleanrooms trace back to design decisions made years earlier. These are the most frequently encountered mistakes in real Singapore MNC project experience:

  • !
    Insufficient door undercut sizing — Too small an undercut means excessive resistance to door opening and abnormal pressure spikes. Too large increases leakage flow and makes pressurisation harder to maintain. The correct size must be calculated per room, not guessed.
  • !
    Not accounting for door open scenarios — The control system must be designed to maintain cascade integrity even when multiple doors are open simultaneously. Design engineers who only calculate steady-state pressurisation without considering dynamic door-open events will create systems that fail during normal operations.
  • !
    Ignoring penetration leakage — Every pipe, duct, conduit, and cable tray passing through cleanroom walls is a potential leakage path. Penetration sealing details must be included in the design, specified, and verified during construction — they are consistently overlooked on busy construction sites.
  • !
    Pressure sensor placed too close to supply diffusers — A DPT sensor in the turbulent zone near a supply grille will read erroneously due to dynamic pressure effects. Sensors should be placed on stable wall surfaces away from direct airflow paths, at a height of 1.2–1.5m.
  • !
    No pressure bump testing during commissioning — The only way to validate a cascade design is to physically test it — measure airflow through door gaps, confirm alarm setpoints trigger correctly, and test system response to door-open events. Projects that skip this step during commissioning discover the problems during qualification — at far greater cost and delay.
  • !
    Not designing for process exhaust variability — In semiconductor fabs, process exhaust flows vary significantly depending on which tools are running. A pressurisation design that works perfectly at 100% tool utilisation may fail at partial utilisation if the control system cannot track the changing exhaust demand. VAV control strategies must be validated across the full operational envelope.
From the Field

In 25+ years of cleanroom projects across Singapore and the APAC region, the most common pressurisation failures during qualification are not caused by wrong design calculations — they are caused by correct calculations being poorly communicated to the construction team, leading to execution gaps. Design intent documentation and construction supervision are as important as the engineering calculations themselves.
