01

Why Load Calculation Matters

Every HVAC system begins with a number — the total heat load. Get this number wrong and everything downstream is wrong: the chiller is undersized and the cleanroom overheats, or oversized and wastes millions of dollars in capital cost and energy. In mission critical facilities, the consequences are far more severe than in commercial buildings.

Load calculation in mission critical environments is significantly more complex than standard building HVAC because the dominant heat sources are not people and sunlight — they are high-density process equipment, lighting systems, and airflow itself. These loads are larger, more variable, and harder to predict than anything in conventional building design.

Key Principle

In a typical office building, process equipment contributes less than 20% of the total cooling load. In a semiconductor fab or pharmaceutical cleanroom, process equipment and lighting can contribute 70–85% of the total load. This fundamentally changes how load calculations are approached for mission critical facilities.

02

The 5 Categories of HVAC Load

Every HVAC load in a mission critical facility falls into one of five categories. Understanding each category — what causes it, how it is measured, and how it behaves — is the foundation of accurate load calculation.

Load Category Sources Type Magnitude in Cleanrooms
Process Equipment Semiconductor tools, pharmaceutical reactors, data center servers Sensible Very High
Lighting Cleanroom luminaires, yellow-light zones, UV systems Sensible Medium
People (Occupancy) Operators, maintenance technicians, engineers Sensible + Latent Low–Medium
Fan Heat Gain RAHU fans, FFU motors, supply fan systems Sensible Medium
Envelope & Infiltration Wall conduction, roof solar gain, infiltration through gaps Sensible + Latent Low (well-insulated)
Outside Air (Ventilation) Make-up air for pressurisation and ventilation (MAU load) Sensible + Latent High (Singapore climate)
💡 Singapore Context

Singapore's hot and humid tropical climate (ambient conditions of ~32°C dry bulb, ~27°C wet bulb) means the outside air load handled by the Make-Up Air Unit (MAU) is a dominant component — especially the latent (dehumidification) load. This is a major differentiator from temperate-climate facility design and must not be underestimated.

03

Sensible Load — Heat Sources in Detail

Sensible heat is heat that raises the dry bulb temperature of the air. It is what most engineers think of when they think of "cooling load." In cleanrooms, sensible load is almost entirely dominated by internal gains — equipment, lighting, and fans — rather than external gains from the building envelope.

  • →
    Process tool heat load — The single largest contributor in semiconductor fabs. Each tool's heat dissipation must be obtained from the equipment vendor data sheet. Critical point: not all electrical power consumed by a tool becomes heat load to the HVAC system. Some is consumed by the process (e.g. heating wafers) and some is exhausted via the process exhaust system. The HVAC-relevant fraction is typically 40–70% of the tool's total power draw, but this varies significantly and must be confirmed with the tool vendor.
  • →
    Lighting heat load — Cleanroom lighting is typically high-intensity LED luminaires (3,000–5,000K, 500–1,000 lux at working plane). Lighting load is calculated from the total wattage of all fixtures. 100% of electrical power consumed by luminaires becomes sensible heat to the cleanroom — LEDs are more efficient but still generate significant heat at the densities required for cleanroom work.
  • →
    Fan motor heat gain — RAHU fans and FFU motors add heat to the airstream. This is often overlooked by junior engineers. The motor efficiency and whether the motor is inside or outside the airstream both affect how this is calculated. Typically 3–8 kW per large RAHU fan unit, multiplied by 10–30 RAHUs in a large fab — this becomes significant.
  • →
    People heat gain — Operators in a cleanroom in full gowning suits generate approximately 130–180W of sensible heat each, slightly more than in office environments due to the insulating effect of cleanroom garments. Occupancy levels vary significantly by time of day and must be assessed for both peak and typical scenarios.
  • →
    UPS and power distribution — Uninterruptible power supply (UPS) systems and power distribution units (PDUs) in cleanrooms have efficiency losses of 3–8% that become heat loads in the space. Often forgotten in early-stage calculations.

"The most common load calculation error I have seen on new engineer submissions is using 100% of tool nameplate power as the HVAC load. The nameplate power is the maximum electrical consumption — the actual HVAC heat contribution is always lower, and getting this wrong can lead to a 30–50% overestimate of cooling plant size."

04

Key Formulas

These are the fundamental equations used in every cleanroom HVAC load calculation. Mastering these relationships — and understanding the assumptions behind each variable — is the starting point for all HVAC sizing work.

Sensible Cooling Load
Q_sensible = ṁ × Cp × ΔT
Q Sensible heat load (kW)
ṁ Mass flow rate of air (kg/s)
Cp Specific heat of air = 1.006 kJ/kg·K
ΔT Temperature difference between supply and return air (°C)
Practical version: Q (kW) = Airflow (m³/s) × 1.2 (air density) × 1.006 × ΔT. For a cleanroom with 10 m³/s supply air and ΔT of 8°C → Q = 10 × 1.2 × 1.006 × 8 = 96.6 kW sensible cooling.
Latent Cooling Load (Dehumidification)
Q_latent = ṁ × hfg × Δω
Q Latent heat load (kW)
ṁ Mass flow rate of air (kg/s)
hfg Latent heat of vaporisation of water ≈ 2,501 kJ/kg
Δω Difference in humidity ratio (kg water/kg dry air)
Singapore outside air at 27°C wb has ω ≈ 0.0187 kg/kg. Cleanroom setpoint at 45% RH, 21°C has ω ≈ 0.0071 kg/kg. Δω = 0.0116 kg/kg — a large dehumidification load handled entirely by the MAU.
Total Cooling Load (Room)
Q_total = Q_equipment + Q_lighting + Q_people + Q_fans + Q_envelope + Q_OA
Each component is calculated separately and summed. A diversity factor (typically 0.7–0.9) is then applied to equipment loads to account for the fact that not all tools operate at peak simultaneously. The OA load (Q_OA) is handled by the MAU and is calculated separately from the room recirculation load.
Chilled Water Flow Rate
ṁ_chw = Q_total ÷ (Cp_water × ΔT_chw)
ṁ_chw Chilled water flow rate (kg/s or L/s)
Cp_water Specific heat of water = 4.187 kJ/kg·K
ΔT_chw Chilled water supply/return differential (typically 5–7°C)
Example: 500 kW total load, ΔT_chw = 6°C → ṁ_chw = 500 ÷ (4.187 × 6) = 19.9 kg/s = ~19.9 L/s = ~71.6 m³/hr chilled water flow.
Sensible Heat Ratio (SHR)
SHR = Q_sensible ÷ (Q_sensible + Q_latent)
SHR tells you how much of the total load is sensible vs latent. Semiconductor cleanrooms typically have SHR of 0.85–0.95 (high sensible, low latent internally). The MAU handles most of the latent component from outside air. Pharmaceutical Grade B/C cleanrooms may have lower SHR due to stringent humidity requirements and higher people density.
05

Latent Load — Humidity Control in Singapore

The latent (moisture) load is the heat associated with water vapour in the air. Removing moisture requires more energy than simply cooling air, and in Singapore's tropical climate, the latent load on the Make-Up Air Unit (MAU) is one of the most critical — and most energy-intensive — aspects of cleanroom HVAC design.

Singapore Outside Air
Typical design conditions
Dry Bulb Temp32–34°C
Wet Bulb Temp27–28°C
Relative Humidity~80–90%
Humidity Ratio ω0.0187 kg/kg
Enthalpy~80 kJ/kg
Semiconductor Cleanroom Target
Typical design conditions
Dry Bulb Temp21 ± 0.5°C
Relative Humidity40–45% RH
Humidity Ratio ω0.0065 kg/kg
Enthalpy~37 kJ/kg
Δω from OA0.0122 kg/kg
Pharma Grade B Target
Typical design conditions
Dry Bulb Temp20 ± 2°C
Relative Humidity45–55% RH
Humidity Ratio ω0.0078 kg/kg
Enthalpy~40 kJ/kg
Δω from OA0.0109 kg/kg

The large difference in humidity ratio between Singapore outside air and cleanroom targets means the MAU must perform significant overcooling and reheating — cooling the air well below the dew point to condense out moisture, then reheating it to the required supply temperature. This process is energy-intensive and must be carefully accounted for in the total plant load.

⚠️ Common Design Error

Junior engineers sometimes calculate only the room sensible load and size the chiller based on that number alone — completely ignoring the latent dehumidification load on the MAU. In Singapore conditions, the MAU latent load can be 30–60% of the total cooling plant requirement. Omitting it leads to a significantly undersized chiller plant that cannot maintain humidity setpoints during operations.

06

Worked Example — Semiconductor Cleanroom Bay

The following is a simplified worked example of an HVAC load calculation for a single semiconductor cleanroom process bay. Real calculations involve far more detail, but this demonstrates the methodology and the relative magnitude of each load component.

Worked Example
ISO Class 5 Semiconductor Process Bay — Singapore Fab
Given Conditions: Room size: 20m × 15m × 4m height  |  8 process tools × 50 kW nameplate each  |  HVAC fraction: 60%  |  Lighting: 60 W/m²  |  Occupancy: 10 persons  |  2 RAHUs × 7.5 kW fan motors  |  Room setpoint: 21°C / 45% RH
1
Process Equipment Load
8 tools × 50 kW × 60% HVAC fraction = 240 kW
Q_equipment = 240 kW
40% of tool power exits via process exhaust — not an HVAC load
2
Lighting Load
20m × 15m × 60 W/m² = 18,000 W = 18 kW
Q_lighting = 18 kW
100% of lighting electrical power becomes sensible heat
3
People Load
10 persons × 130 W/person = 1,300 W = 1.3 kW
Q_people = 1.3 kW (sensible) + ~0.5 kW (latent)
Minor contributor — cleanroom occupancy is typically low relative to equipment load
4
RAHU Fan Motor Heat Gain
2 RAHUs × 7.5 kW × 85% motor efficiency factor = 12.75 kW
Q_fans = 12.75 kW
Fan motor inefficiency heat enters the airstream — always include this
5
Envelope Load (well-insulated cleanroom)
Estimated at 5 kW (interior room, no external walls)
Q_envelope = 5 kW
Low — cleanrooms are typically interior spaces with well-insulated panel systems
6
Total Room Sensible Cooling Load
240 + 18 + 1.3 + 12.75 + 5 = 277 kW
Apply diversity factor 0.85 → 277 × 0.85 = 235 kW
Q_room_sensible = 235 kW (after diversity)
This is the load on the RAHU cooling coils — NOT the total chiller plant load
7
MAU Outside Air Load (Singapore conditions)
OA flow = 5,000 m³/hr = 1.39 m³/s  |  Enthalpy difference = 80 – 37 = 43 kJ/kg
MAU load = 1.39 × 1.2 × 43 = 71.7 kW
Q_MAU = 71.7 kW (sensible + latent combined)
Handled by MAU cooling coil — separate from RAHU load but adds to total chiller demand
8
Total Chiller Plant Load (This Bay)
235 kW (room) + 71.7 kW (MAU) = 306.7 kW
Total = ~307 kW ≈ 87 Refrigeration Tonnes (RT)
1 RT = 3.517 kW. A real fab has 20–100+ bays — total chiller plant in MW range
📐 Scale Perspective

This single bay example produces ~87 RT of cooling demand. A large semiconductor fab in Singapore with 50 process bays of similar loading would require approximately 4,000–5,000 RT of total chiller plant capacity — equivalent to the cooling system of a large commercial shopping centre, dedicated to a single cleanroom facility.

Common Calculation Mistakes

These are the most frequently encountered errors in HVAC load calculations submitted by junior engineers in Singapore MNC project environments:

  • !
    Using nameplate power = HVAC load — Process tool nameplate electrical power is not equal to the HVAC cooling load. A significant fraction exits via process exhaust. Always obtain the HVAC heat dissipation value from the tool vendor's Facilities Requirements Document (FRD), not the nameplate.
  • !
    Ignoring MAU latent load — Calculating only room sensible load and omitting the MAU outside air dehumidification load leads to severe underestimation of total chiller plant requirement. In Singapore conditions this can be 20–40% of total plant load.
  • !
    Not applying a diversity factor — Not all process tools run at 100% simultaneously. Applying a diversity factor of 0.75–0.85 to equipment loads gives a more realistic peak load. Omitting this leads to costly over-sizing of cooling plant.
  • !
    Forgetting fan heat gain — RAHU and FFU fan motors add heat to the airstream. In high air change rate cleanrooms with many RAHUs, this can add 5–10% to the total sensible load and must be included.
  • !
    Using wrong air density — Air density varies with temperature and altitude. At Singapore conditions (sea level, ~24°C average), air density is approximately 1.18–1.20 kg/m³. Using the standard 1.2 kg/m³ is acceptable; using 1.293 kg/m³ (standard conditions at 0°C) overestimates airflow-based loads.
  • !
    Not separating MAU load from RAHU load — The MAU handles the outside air conditioning load. The RAHU handles the room recirculation load. These are served by different coils and sometimes different chilled water circuits. Mixing them into a single number makes equipment selection impossible.
  • !
    Ignoring future load growth — Cleanroom facilities are designed for 10–20 year operational life. Tool populations change, processes evolve, and power densities increase. A good load calculation includes a future capacity margin — typically 20–30% — agreed with the client at the Basis of Design stage.
From the Field

In real Singapore MNC projects, the load calculation document goes through multiple review cycles — owner's engineer review, peer review, and sometimes third-party independent verification for large facilities. Learning to document assumptions clearly, show all calculation steps, and flag uncertainties is as important as getting the numbers right. A well-documented calculation that is slightly conservative is far more valuable than a poorly documented calculation that happens to be accurate.
